Social Impact of Climate Change

The U.S. should prioritize immigration policy, especially with consideration of the nation’s contribution to climate change and the immigration that climate disasters cause. The world’s undeveloped southern hemisphere faces the most damaging effects of increasing global temperatures, while the industrialized northern hemisphere contributes the most emissions. As undeveloped regions become less inhabitable due to extreme weather and natural disasters, people continue to migrate to the protection of developed regions. As global temperatures rise and the impacts of climate change become increasingly irreversible, action becomes increasingly necessary. Climate denial occurs to varying degrees: denial of its existence, damage, priority, and proper mitigation. A case study on Bangladesh from the University of Hamburg and a case study on El Salvador from the United Nations both display the unequal effects of climate damage, the damage-driven migration, and the increasing need for action.

The academic paper Climate Change, Migration, and Displacement: The Underestimated Disaster separates forty pages into an analysis of the climate-migration dynamic, an explanation of the factors, and a set of case studies, all of which support its conclusion: “[T]he planet will continue to experience natural disasters which do not (yet) affect some of us, but bring great suffering to the poorest of the poor who are the least to blame for their occurrence. We can simply no longer afford to continue to underestimate and ignore these catastrophic events” (34). The analysis of the climate-migration dynamic, “Climate Change, Migration Patterns, and Decisions on Migration,” uses definition of terms including “risk,” “climate,” and “migration” to explain the complexity of both migration decisions and their connections with climate change (id. 8, 10, 11). In explaining climate factors and migration, the passage increases in intentional meaning and decreases in extensional meaning by introducing such terms as “sudden natural disaster,” “gradual changes,” and “climate measures” (id. 12, 15, 18). The case studies are the most effective premises, supporting the passage’s central conclusion using inductive inferences. 

The United Nation World Food Program released the report Food Security and Emigration: Why people flee and the impact on family members left behind in El Salvador, Guatemala, and Honduras in 2019. The passage is an extended argument arranged in a vertical pattern. Residents of the Dry Corridor migrate due to food shortages; climate conditions in the Dry Corridor cause unemployment and poor working conditions; poor working conditions, adverse climate, and violence cause food shortages and thus migration; therefore, national and international groups need to reduce these “push-factors” (id. 6, 21). The circumstances that Climate Change, Migration, and Displacement highlights in Bangladesh alongside the state of El Salvador illustrated in Food Security and Emigration , work together to display the unequal effects of climate damage, the damage-driven migration, and the increasing need for action.

Climate denial occurs to varying degrees: denial of its existence, damage, priority, and proper mitigation. While the climate has experienced periods of change in the past, statistical evidence shows that hydrocarbon emissions in the past seventy years correlate to an irregular increase in global temperatures compared to trends over the past 400,000 years (UCR). Moreover, the rise in global temperature and hydrocarbons is changing global habitats and increasing extreme weather events. “Mean sea level has been rising at an average rate of 1.7 mm/year (plus or minus 0.5mm) over the past 100 years, which is significantly larger than the rate averaged over the last several thousand years” (UCR). Long-term historical records, simulated climate models, and reliable consideration of meteorology attribute increases in extreme weather to climate change despite the difficulties of classifying and determining the causes of climate events (CEWECC 105).

The world’s undeveloped southern hemisphere faces the most damaging effects of increasing global temperatures, while the industrialized northern hemisphere contributes the most emissions. One of the most illustrative case studies in Climate Change, Migration, and Displacement examines Bangladesh, a country responsible for 0. 06% of global emissions yet one of the most impacted by climate change (Bedarff and Jakobeit 19). One-third of the country lies below tidal level with a population of 35 million (id.). An average of one-fifth of the country is flooded annually (id.). A sea-level rise of one meter, the estimated average by 2100, would force one million people out of their homes (id.). Bangladesh is also plagued by heat and drought, which will also become more severe with rising global temperatures, making the cultivation of rice no longer possible in parts of the country (id.). The problem, however, is not as significant for developed countries as it is for countries with low incomes: “In Japan, England, the Netherlands, and other low-lying countries, architects and civil engineers have developed promising technologies for flood control” (Craven).

The distinction between developed countries and undeveloped countries is sometimes unclear, approaching a fallacious false dilemma. Nations like India and China participate in the post-industrial global economy, but large portions of the population are not urban. In 2013, India emitted 2.4 billion metric tons (GT) of carbon dioxide, a rate behind only China with 10.0 GT and the United States with 5.2 GT (Charles 54). However, as of 2019, The World Bank reports that the percent of total urban population in India was 34, in China was 60, and in the U.S. was 82. China and India, with populations of over 1.3 billion each, are also significantly larger than the United States with a population of just over 0.3 billion (id.). In such cases of gaps within the populations of countries, for the purpose of this paper, the developed regions can be treated separate from the undeveloped regions, even if the country as a whole appears to statistically contradict the claim that undeveloped regions are harmed the most by climate change despite contributing the least.

As undeveloped regions become less inhabitable due to natural disasters and gradual, extreme weather, people continue to migrate to the protection of developed regions. Due to poor rainfall and droughts linked to the El Niño effect, household food production in El Salvador has been declining further in recent years (Bedarff and Jakobeit 17). The drought conditions of El Niño also resulted in a significant increase in irregular migration to the USA beginning in 2014 (United Nations 5). Still, “The search for better livelihoods motivates people to migrate, but poor people, in particular, do not often have the financial means to do so” (Bedarff and Jakobeit 10). Researchers at the University of Hamburg found that extreme weather hinders the movement of migrant communities and consequently puts these communities in increasingly precarious positions as they lose sufficient resources required to finance a journey (id. 6). More broadly, the risk to humans of being displaced through sudden natural disasters is 60 percent higher today than it was forty years ago (id. 18).

Worsening conditions for agriculture also cause climate displacement. Over the last three decades fertile soils and water have become scarcer in many areas and, a process that continues as temperatures rise (id. 17). For example, high unemployment, minimal demand for jobs, and low, irregular wages are typical in El Salvador’s Dry Corridor. Over half of the interviewed households reported spending more than two-thirds of their income on food, suggesting a high level of economic vulnerability to food insecurity (United Nations 5). Adverse climatic ruin opportunities for agricultural work but also agricultural productivity as a whole in both commercial and subsistence agricultures (id.). 

Bangladesh also illustrates the poor trade-off people make in trying to escape such environmental damage. Today, 850 million people already live in slums (Bedarff and Jakobeit 30). Legally, “Slum means any area where dwellings predominate which by reason of dilapidation, overcrowding, faulty arrangement or design, lack of ventilation, light or sanitary facilities, or any combination of these factors, are detrimental to safety, health and morals” (Law Insider). In thirty years opportunities for improved employment, education, infrastructure, health care, and protection against persecution could increase the number of slum residents to three billion (Bedarff and Jakobeit 30). 

As global temperatures rise and the impacts of climate change become increasingly irreversible, action becomes increasingly necessary. The need for action is clear. Climate change has already caused droughts: ruining agriculture, floods: damaging infrastructure, and gradual changes: taking communities’ flora, fauna, and farms (Bedarff and Jakobeit 13, 16, 21, 23, 25). Such disasters violate fundamental rights: “the rights to life, personal freedom and security, food, housing, water, health, and education” (id. 7). Therefore, climate change deprives people of their fundamental rights. The IDMC method for determining risk considers the degree of destruction caused by natural disasters, the degree of human exposure, and the human ability to adapt (Bedarff and Jakobeit 8). Climate change increases the degree of destruction by increasing temperature and decreasing precipitation; it increases the degree of exposure by changing weather patterns; it decreases the human ability to react by limiting resources (id. 9). 

The U.S. should prioritize immigration policy, especially with consideration of the nation’s contribution to climate change and the immigration that climate disasters cause. The importance of this priority is clearly demonstrated by unequal effects of climate damage, damage-driven migration, and increasing need for action.
