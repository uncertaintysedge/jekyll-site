College Education Provides Tools for Freedom
While attending high school, it is difficult not to feel stagnant, aimless, or anxious. Many high school activities are made to better the individual so that they can better contribute to the collective, but such activities neither provide time for individuals to directly and actively contribute to a community or cause, nor provide reasons for individuals to strive for anything more than the ability to follow rules, do work, and maintain a reputation. A college education is an opportunity for students to build for themselves freedom, meaning, and fluidity in life.
In the Autobiography of Frederick Douglas, Douglas dedicates his freedom to his education. He describes learning to read from boys in the street and realizing the atrocity of his slavery. From there Douglas went on to play a significant role in the fight against slavery. Through education, he not only improved his quality of life, but he was also able to improve other people’s lives. College is an opportunity to do the same. It is a place to seek understanding, to be educated and prepared to help those in need.
In contrast to Douglas, Jean-Paul Sartre describes absolute freedom in The Transcendence of the Ego: “She found herself monstrously free, and this vertiginous freedom appeared to her at the opportunity for this action which she was afraid of doing” (100). Sartre’s existential views lead him to fear the complete control he had over his life. A college education is a way to meet and combat this extreme freedom by meeting uncertainty with academic and social experience. Sartre's absolute freedom was a cause of anxiety. But anxiety often arises from uncertainty, which can be overcome with experience and education.
College also addresses the issue of nihilism, an extreme form of skepticism that denies all truth. Greek Sceptic Sextus Empiricus wrote Pros Mathematikous, or Against the Professors, by Sextus Empiricus is a second-century Greek philosophical text that is one of the most thorough surviving records of ancient skepticism. This piece is included in several volume collections of writings that question accepted knowledge; Against the Professors specifically criticizes grammar, rhetoric, geometry, arithmetic, astrology, and music. For most nihilists, the most rational personal decision is to die or murder; classic examples include Columbine Shooter Eric Harris and biblical figure Cain (Harris; The Catholic Teen Bible Genesis 4).
Life is a fight against death. It is commonly accepted that the alternative to eating, sleeping, and socializing is pain or death. If a person has no reason to fight against these necessities, they are prone to despair and misanthropy. The American Psychiatric Association cites deviations from these needs as some of the primary symptoms of Major Depressive Disorder (Diagnostic…). Thus, it is clear that life has a purpose at its most fundamental level, as life without purpose is death, but education can change a life of survival into much more.
College, like high school, creates a potential for the individual that is often expressed as hope. Adulthood becomes a realized series of choices to accept the state of being or strive to experience different influences and environments, to encounter unique individuals, cultures, and ideas. Through learning a person becomes more familiar with the nuances of the universe. While nihilism acknowledges that lack of human understanding, it also fails to acknowledge human potential.
A college education provides the tools to strive for purpose, even given acknowledgment of how limited human understanding is. In the Apology of Socrates, Plato records Socrates as saying:
… the god is really wise and by his oracle means this: ‘Human wisdom is of little or no value.’ And it appears that he does not really say this of Socrates, but merely uses my name, and makes me an example, as if he were to say: "The one of you human beings is wisest who, like Socrates, recognizes that he is in truth of no account in respect to wisdom (9).
More simply, understanding starts with the acknowledgment of human understanding’s many shortcomings. Moreover, Aristotle is more popularly quoted from the Apology for saying, “...the unexamined life is not worth living…” (25); the preservation of life relies on philosophy. In parallel concentration camp survivor Victor Frankel wrote:
Ultimately, man should not ask what the meaning of his life is, but rather must recognize that it is he who is asked. In a word, each man is questioned by life; and he can only answer to life by answering for his own life; to life he can only respond by being responsible (49).
Notice the word “recognize” from Socrates and the word “examine” from Frankel.  These words suggest a continuous process of prudence, experience, and reflection, a process that is not inherent in the every-day modern life but can, nonetheless, be readily pursued in college.
College provides freedom and purpose, which give way to fluidity. Coming out of high school, teenagers are expected to have a fairly developed idea of what they want to do with the rest of their lives. A teenager is supposed to know their motivation. College, however, is both a bridge to and a major piece of the rest of their lives, where young adults can seek meaning, and thus find motivation. Education turns a life of survival into an abundance of time to appreciate beauty and pain, truth and deceit, love and selfishness. A college education is a catalyst that changes the person so that they can then change other people, communities, and the world to come.
High school is a microcosm of the real world, putting people in social and occupational environments. However, it is also supposed to spark curiosity, providing a college-bound impetus and allowing students to magnify the microcosm high school experience into a series of interactions with the world as a whole. Freedom, meaning, and fluidity in life await.
