The role of a psychologist is to determine how people think from observations of their behavior. In Anthem, Ayn Rand's narrator reverses the role of a  psychologist, so that behavior expresses the mental state of the character Equality 7-2521, later called Prometheus. This is Prometheus’ sequence of behaviors and corresponding thought processes: first  Prometheus’ curiosity as a child is evidence of his individualism, then,  his role as a street sweeper reflects his collectivism, and next, his work in the tunnel indicates his individualism. The most pivotal of these scenes is Prometheus walking out of his unguarded prison cell. This scene has two primary aspects. It represents Prometheus’ way of thinking and his relationship to the society he lives in, and it defines the role of freedom for the individual in contrast to freedom for the collective.
Societies use prisons for limitation and isolation. In Rand’s collective society, everybody relies on everybody else. Each person is assigned their role to contribute to the whole by “The Council of Vocations” (Anthem). A proper member of a collective society would have no one to help or receive help from in isolation and would lose their impetus. Moreover, a collective society would have no need for prison because the will of each individual coincides with the general welfare. Rand says directly in the text, “There is no reason to have guards, for men have never defied the Councils so far as to escape from whatever place they were ordered to be” (Anthem). This concept is expanded on in her essay “The Soul of the Collectivist:”
A world where the thought of each man will not be his own but an attempt to guess the thought in the brain of his neighbor who’ll have no thought of his own...where no man will hold a desire for himself, but will direct his effort to satisfy the desires of his neighbor who’ll have no desires.
Ironically, the existence of the prison is evidence of flaws in the society Prometheus is part of. It is evident that a higher power wants to maintain a way of life that can benefit from isolating noncollectivist individuals.
The prison is not only evidence that the society Prometheus lives in is flawed, but also of how it is flawed. Prior to throwing Prometheus into prison, society has controlled him by influencing the way he thinks.  Again, in “The Soul of the Collectivist,” the  character Ellsworth M. Toohey describes how he will rule humanity using the individual against himself, so he destroys his own goals and reasoning, altering his beliefs. Toohey claims,  “Just say that reason is limited, that there is something above it. What? You don’t have to be too clear about it either.” Anthem’s prison has no guards because Prometheus’ only restraint is the mindset he has learned. Nonetheless, by thinking differently than the collective, he is able to escape. This method of control is comparable to the influence of religion in theocratic governments like ancient Rome, Tudor England, and contemporary Iran. The people do not need to rebel against a personified power because the rules they follow are ingrained in their way of thinking.
Who is responsible for the error of a counter-intuitive prison in the collectivist society? The most influential people seen in Anthem are the Council, who are described as old and lifeless: “Their hair was white and their faces were cracked as the clay of a dry river bed. They were old.” Through Prometheus, Rand does not describe anything even resembling political or economical conflict. The descriptions, instead, use a dull and lifeless diction as a reflection of the elders. An example is the scene before Prometheus finds the tunnel:
It is empty save for trees and weeds. Beyond the ravine there is a plain, and beyond the plain there lies the Uncharted Forest, about which men must not think. We were gathering the papers and the rags which the wind had blown from the Theatre when we saw an iron bar among the weeds. It was old and rusted by many rains (Anthem).
This prison exposes the Council’s opposition to how Prometheus acts and thinks, and in doing so helps to illustrate the society’s priorities in contrast to his. It seems the government has traded war and poverty for lifelessness. Rand seems to think freedom is a major component to humanity, and she defines freedom through her ideas about oppression. She repeatedly communicates that reliance on other people ruins freedom. For example, when Prometheus escapes to his house in the forest he says, “There is nothing to take a man's freedom away from him, save other men. To be free, a man must be free of his brothers” (Anthem).
        By using the prison as an interaction between society and Prometheus, Rand is able to contrast Prometheus’ individualism with the ideas of his collectivist society by defining the power of freedom for the individual in contrast to the dull safeties of uniformity.
