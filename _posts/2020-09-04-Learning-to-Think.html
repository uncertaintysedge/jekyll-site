4 September 2019
Learning to Think
        Schools in the United States prepare children to memorize and to work together, even promoting preparation to ‘think critically,’ but not to live meaningful lives or evaluate and solve personal and societal issues, two issues that are made important by the way truth, money, and other people are treated.
The U.S. Department of Education’s mission is to “promote student achievement and preparation for global competitiveness by fostering educational excellence and ensuring equal access” (“About ED”). Education is also considered a basic human right that lifts marginalized groups out of poverty, promotes understanding of other rights, and contributes to the full development of the human personality (“Understanding…”). Nonetheless, high school education in the U.S. introduces students to general subjects by teaching them to memorize and to problem solve and to support extracurricular activities that foster discipline, teamwork, and identity. These are beneficial skills for a citizen of the United States looking to work in one of the many corporate areas, but they do not account for some of the basic aspects of human need. Ideas on teaching and learning date back to Plato, who saw education as an equal opportunity for competition and development of individual justice, which he argued requires knowledge. He further claimed that these aspects of education make possible a positive relationship among all social classes (Lee). Schooling in the U.S. provides an environment for humans to socialize and become familiar with the accepted ideals of society so that they can be functioning members. This preparation to contribute to society was first exemplified by the German and Prussian schooling systems that U.S. schooling systems were originally based on; then in the first colonies where education was a means for the transition of agrarian children to urban citizens; followed by Horrace Mann’s ideas on free, public, mandatory, education divided based on tests scores; and finally the ‘progressive education’ of John Dewey and the PEA (Sass; Gatto). It was not until the mid-20th century that test scores were officially emphasized to ensure equality of education as through the No Child Left Behind Act and 21st Century Skills (“No Child…”; “What are 21st…”).
The current schooling system is capable of filling the lower tiers of Maslow’s hierarchy of needs, including physiological, security, social, and esteem needs, but not the cognitive, self-actualization, and transcendence needs (McLeod). In other words, schooling is meeting needs that if not met would otherwise cause a deficiency, but not fostering all potential growth. Education is one of the surest ways to prepare an individual to contribute to their community and problems larger than themselves. The original purpose of schooling was to prepare young white men to be quality citizens in a mostly capitalist country founded on individual freedom and equal opportunity. Evidence of this original demographic and motivation is seen from the first public school in the U.S., the Boston Latin School, in 1635, up to the first continually operating girls school, Ursuline Academy, in 1727 (“BLS History;” Clark 193). The U.S. strives to offer equal education to citizens of all genders and ethnicities using standardization to measure ‘analytical reasoning’ and ‘problem solving’ despite percentage outlays for the U.S. department of education ranging from 1.2% to 3.5% since the No Child Left Behind Act of 2001 (“No Child…”; “What are 21st…”; “Percentage…”). Critics such as John Taylor Gatto and Ivan Illich claim schooling brainwashes people to live as society wants them to. In a sense they are correct, but only in the extreme. People need to know how to be part of a society, but they also need to know how to contribute to solving personal and societal issues to the best of their personal abilities. Economist Adam Smith spoke of capitalism in saying, “Humans have many ‘higher’ needs… among these, our need for education, for self-understanding, for beautiful cities, and for rewarding social lives” (The School of Life).
Aristotle considered philosophy the basis of understanding. He also viewed logic as the basic means of reasoning, which allows for complete questions and study (Philosophy of Aristotle). In The Outlook for Intelligence poet Paul Valery says, “Our kind of innovation consists not in the answers, but in the true novelty of the questions themselves.” These great thinkers make clear the importance of philosophy to thinking. Philosophy, however, is not only important to “educational excellence,” which the U.S. Department of Education strives for; it is the basis of personal and societal understanding. Regis High School theology teacher, Mr. Jim Tabor, says “My view on philosophy is that it determines everything. For example, what defines a man?” Philosophy questions values and what is defined as fact. Because of this, philosophy helps with major life choices, supports a more thoughtful society, and improves the wellbeing of students.
High school students are faced with many choices that require familiarity with philosophical ideas and reasoning as they develop into adults and . These decisions include areas such as relationships with family, friends, and those in need as adole; the role of the individual in and outside of a community; the importance and dangers of ambition; and the consequences of individual’s actions on the world. These decisions are seen in questions like who to make friends with, why to treat others a certain way, how and why to prioritize friends, family, and personal interests, how many activities an individual wants to participate in outside of school, what classes an individual wants to take, how much, in what way, and an individual wants to challenge themselves, and so on. Up until high school, parents make decisions for their children, so adolescents lack experience with this responsibility. One consequence of such inexperience is denial of volition. People work at entry level jobs because they do not know what they want to do with their lives. From 1993 to 2016 the highest percentage of high school graduates to enroll in college was 69.7% (“69.7 percent”). As technology improves, factory work, physical labor, maintenance, agriculture, and security will no longer be career options for humans who did not have good experiences in school (Baker and Buffie). It will have to be more common to pursue higher education and to consider the innumerable pros and cons associated with it. The understanding of self and society fostered by philosophy is important to making such decisions about debt, education, and occupation. It is especially important in a capitalist society, which allows people to make many choices within what is monetarily possible given time and resources. Students need to learn to prioritize, how to deal with past successes or failures, and how to understand their position in the world.
It is possible that individuals may develop the skills to make important decisions outside of school. This, however, is not ideal. Experience contributes greatly to how an individual perceives the world, but people need a method of organizing these perceptions. This condition is especially true in a world of biased individuals and relative truth. Anybody looking for a straight answer will encounter a different one from family members, religious groups, political parties, the news, social media,  all at once. Providing philosophy in school creates ground for providing ideas that can be compared and contrasted with as little bias as possible. Proponents of relativism and absolute truth can both benefit from understanding ideas of past and current thinkers. It has often become the case that people want to perceive others as beneath themselves, so they try to belittle them. Then the person who has received the hate plays the victim. This aggression and self pity have made discussion improbable. How can people talk about serious issues, when they are determined to hold onto their beliefs?
Most individuals have a difficult time separating people’s ideas from the people themselves. The discomfort this creates and the common seriousness of these issues make them an unpopular topic for conversation between friends and family. An organized philosophy class for adolescents might help people deal with personal and societal problems. Only 60% of the 450 million people with mental health conditions worldwide receive any care (Friedman). If this is any reflection of the world’s remaining seven billion people, it is seemingly unpopular to share internal struggles. Most people use distractions such as movies, video games, and social media to compensate for this lack of understanding and meaning. Popular music increasingly produces themes and topics about vague love or vague wealth (Christenson).
Entertainment and distraction have become synonymous. The term “attention economy,” as opposed to the textbook popular “information economy,” argues that information is not a limited resource, but attention is, and companies want it. Facebook and Google have ads tailored to the individual. Netflix and other streaming services recommend shows based on watch history and allow episodes to play one after another seamlessly. People refer to these things as entertainment, but individuals who get home from work, sit on the couch, and turn on the television to play a continuous stream of TV show episodes while they scroll through social media on their phones, they are not looking to be entertained. Schools, however, are not for-profit institutions.
The ED also describes “preparation for global competitiveness.”  The Global Competitiveness Report happens yearly to “assess the ability of countries to provide high levels of prosperity to their citizens” (web.archive.org). It does this assessment through:
institutions; appropriate infrastructure; stable macroeconomic framework; good health and primary education; higher education and training; efficient goods markets; efficient labor markets; developed financial markets; ability to harness existing technology;market size, both domestic and international; production of new and different goods using the most sophisticated production processes; innovation (Sala-i-Martín, et al)
If prosperity solely refers to wealth and assets, these variables would provide an accurate representation. These measurements include Adam Smith’s ‘education and beautiful cities,’ but not self knowledge or meaningful relationships. Data compiled from World Bank and Bourguignon and Morrison found a change of people making less than $1.90 per day internationally from 10% in 1820 to 90% in 2015 (Roser). This data support an increase in economic prosperity, but a survey taken by Cigna in 2019 found that 64% of people around the world work in an “always-on culture,” causing stress and adversely affecting both physical and mental well-being (Cigna). In short, people generally leave poverty to live a life that goes nowhere fast. The U.S. Department of Education has a strong connection with the economy, preparing people to contribute their lives to compete and gather wealth, but individual’s emotional needs are not being met. People will be able to live more meaningful lives if they decided decide that there is more to life than earning and spending money. Although the U.S. has no physical tyrant to terrorize the masses, as with Hitler’s Russia, Maoist China, and Stalin’s Russia, people are controlled by money. Economics is defined as the science of how people satisfy seemingly unlimited wants with limited resources (“What is Economics?”). The U.S. economy does this with a constant flow of short term distractions. Television and Movie streaming services cost around $10 a month. Different companies offer new $50 to $1,000 smartphones every year. Fashion changes completely every ten years and shifts every season. Cars range from $2,000 to $10,000,000 based on how much money people want to get rid of. Adam Smith argued that luxury will promote the middle class and promote overall welfare, which has been proven true in practice (The School of Life). However, what is the cost, and is materialism the only solution? In George Orwell’s 1984, war is used to influence thought through the news, and also used to facilitate the production and consumption of materials and labor. The U.S. uses frivolity instead of war. The economy is in a good place, but a meaningless wealthy existence is just as bad as an existence in poverty.
The U.S. Declaration of Independence says,” from that equal creation they derive rights inherent and inalienable, among which are the preservation of life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness." Socrates is popularly quoted for saying, “the unexamined life is not worth living” (Plato 25). This preservation of life relies on philosophy. In his book Man’s Search for Meaning, Victor Frankel says:
Ultimately, man should not ask what the meaning of his life is, but rather must recognize that it is he who is asked. In a word, each man is questioned by life; and he can only answer to life by answering for his own life; to life he can only respond by being responsible (49).
Man not only has the right to liberty, but the duty to act responsibly. Notice the word “recognize” from Aristotle and the word “examine” from Frankel.  These words suggest a continuous process of thought and reflection, which is something missing in schools. Adolescents need to learn how to evaluate moral decisions through self-reflection and comparisons of ideas because of the many future-impacting decisions that are part of high school. The U.S. needs people who are more aware of how to live meaningful lives for the sake of prosperity that is unrelated to wealth and assets. The stigma of personal thought and moral crisis around the world needs to be broken to improve how individuals deal with their problems and the problems of society. For all of this, philosophy needs to be required in high school, and not the philosophy that college graduates discuss in cafes; schools need a philosophy that prepares the individual to be a member of society more fully than personal finance. Schools need a philosophy curriculum that creates individuals who can participate in American democracy.
